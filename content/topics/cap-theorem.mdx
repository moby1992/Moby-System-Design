The **CAP theorem** says that when a distributed system hits a network problem, it must choose between staying *consistent* and staying *available*.

<Layman>

Imagine two ticket booths for the same concert, in two different cities, that normally sync with each other every second.

One day the phone line between them breaks. Now there's one last ticket. Two customers, one at each booth, both want it. The booths can't talk. You face a choice:

- **Play it safe (stay consistent):** both booths refuse to sell until the phone line is fixed, so you never sell the same seat twice — but customers walk away empty-handed.
- **Keep selling (stay available):** both booths sell the ticket and you sort out the mess later — nobody is turned away, but you might double-book.

You genuinely cannot have both while the phone line is down. That's the whole idea of CAP.

</Layman>

<Standard>

CAP states that a distributed data store can simultaneously provide at most **two** of these three guarantees:

- **Consistency (C):** every read receives the most recent write (or an error).
- **Availability (A):** every request receives a non-error response (not necessarily the latest data).
- **Partition tolerance (P):** the system keeps operating despite dropped or delayed messages between nodes.

Because network **partitions are unavoidable** in any real distributed system, *P is not optional*. So the practical choice during a partition is **C vs A**: a system is effectively **CP** or **AP**.

</Standard>

## What this looks like in practice

<Layman>

A bank leans toward "play it safe" — it would rather pause than show you the wrong balance. A social media feed leans toward "keep selling" — it would rather show you a slightly-old feed than show you an error page.

</Layman>

<Standard>

- **CP systems** (e.g. traditional RDBMS, HBase, ZooKeeper) sacrifice availability during a partition to avoid serving stale or conflicting data. Good for financial ledgers and coordination.
- **AP systems** (e.g. Cassandra, DynamoDB, DNS) remain available and reconcile later, typically offering **eventual consistency**. Good for feeds, shopping carts, and telemetry.

A crucial nuance (**PACELC**): even when there's *no* partition (Else), you still trade **Latency vs Consistency** — synchronous replication is consistent but slower; asynchronous is faster but can be stale.

</Standard>

## The takeaway

CAP isn't a menu you pick from once — it's a lens. For each piece of data, decide what matters more *when things go wrong*: never being wrong, or never being down.
